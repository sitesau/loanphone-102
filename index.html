Agricultural Machinery Finance Costs Explained

The specific interest rate offered for equipment finance is not uniform across the industry and relies heavily on the financial health of the farming operation. Established farms with two or more years of strong financials typically secure the most favourable rates, sitting at the lower end of the scale between 6.5 and 9 per cent per annum. These operations present a lower risk to lenders due to their proven track record and ability to service debt through various seasonal cycles. Standard operations trading for one to two years generally fall into a middle tier, with rates ranging between 9 and 12 per cent, while newer or specialist operators may face higher rates between 12 and 15 per cent and above. These figures are indicative as of 2026 and will vary based on individual circumstances, lender assessment and broader market conditions. Understanding how your farm fits into these profiles is essential when reviewing agricultural machinery finance options.

Structure Impacts on Cost

The total cost of acquiring machinery is also influenced by the finance structure selected, as each arrangement handles repayments and ownership differently. A chattel mortgage allows the business to own the asset immediately, while the lender holds a mortgage over it. This structure often suits those wanting to claim the GST input tax credit upfront. In contrast, a commercial hire purchase involves the lender buying the tractor and hiring it to the business, with ownership transferring only after the final payment. Here, the GST is usually claimed over the life of the agreement. A finance lease involves the lender retaining ownership during the term, requiring the business to make rental payments. Each of these structures can accommodate a balloon payment, which is a lump sum due at the end of the term. Including a balloon payment reduces the regular monthly outlay, which can assist with tight monthly cash flow, although it means a larger financial obligation must be met at the conclusion of the loan period.

Tax Implications and Net Cost

For GST-registered businesses, the tax treatment of the finance structure can have a substantial impact on the net cost of the machinery. Under a chattel mortgage, the GST component of the purchase price can typically be claimed as an input tax credit upfront in the business activity statement for the tax period in which the purchase occurs. Additionally, the business can depreciate the asset over its effective life according to ATO guidelines, providing annual deductions that offset taxable income. Under a commercial hire purchase, the GST is generally claimed in instalments over the duration of the loan, and depreciation deductions usually become available once ownership transfers. It is important to note that while interest and charges are often tax-deductible during the agreement, the specific outcomes depend on individual situations and current tax laws. Farmers should confirm these figures with their accountant, as the structure with the lowest monthly repayment is not always the one that delivers the best after-tax result across the life of the asset.

Deposit and Trading History

Lenders assess applications by weighing the trading history of the farm, the type and resale value of the machinery and the proposed deposit. A larger deposit can sometimes mitigate a shorter trading history or a higher risk profile, potentially helping to secure a more competitive interest rate. Established farms with a solid deposit and strong financials are well-positioned to access the lower end of the rate spectrum. However, newer operators should not be discouraged, as finance is still available, though it often comes with higher costs reflecting the increased risk. Common questions

What factors influence the interest rate for farm machinery loans? The primary factors are the farm's trading history and financial strength. Established farms with strong financials usually qualify for lower rates, while newer operations or specialist ventures may face higher rates due to increased risk.

Can a balloon payment reduce agricultural machinery finance? A balloon payment reduces the regular monthly repayment amount, which can help with monthly cash flow management. However, it defers a portion of the cost to the end of the term, requiring a lump sum payment or refinancing at that time.

How does GST affect the overall cost of the equipment? For GST-registered businesses, the GST included in the purchase price is not a sunk cost if it can be claimed as an input tax credit. Structures like chattel mortgages allow this claim upfront, effectively reducing the capital required for the purchase.
