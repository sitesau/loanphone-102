Business Equipment Finance Costs Explained

Interest rates for equipment finance are determined by a combination of the lender's risk appetite, the credit profile of the business and the quality of the asset being purchased. Unlike residential home loans, there is significant variation between lenders, with some specialising in specific industries like construction or agriculture. The market is broad, encompassing major banks and specialised non-bank lenders. Navigating this alone can be difficult, which is why comparison services that access over 100 lenders are valuable. While the interest rate is a major component, the total cost is also influenced by the chosen structure. For example, a chattel mortgage involves the business owning the asset from day one, which has different cash flow implications than an operating lease. To see how these fundamental structures work, business equipment finance outlines the primary options available to Australian companies.

Deposits, Balloons and Loan-to-Value Ratios

Deposits and loan-to-value ratios are critical levers for managing upfront costs. Standard practice in the industry requires a deposit between 10 and 30 per cent. This acts as a safety buffer for the lender and demonstrates the borrower's commitment. However, businesses with a strong trading history and healthy financials can often negotiate up to 100 per cent financing. This effectively removes the need for a deposit, preserving cash for operations. On the other end of the term, borrowers can use a balloon payment. This deferred lump sum allows for lower monthly instalments during the loan term. While this improves monthly cash flow, the borrower must ensure they have the funds or refinancing options available when the balloon falls due.

Tax Implications and Claimable Deductions

The tax treatment of equipment finance is often the deciding factor for business owners. The Australian tax system offers several concessions that can offset the costs of borrowing. Under a chattel mortgage or commercial hire purchase, the business can claim the GST paid on the purchase price as a credit. Furthermore, the interest charged on the loan is generally tax-deductible, reducing the net cost of servicing the debt. Depreciation schedules allow the business to claim the decline in value of the asset over time. Depending on current legislation and turnover thresholds, the instant asset write-off may also apply, allowing the immediate deduction of the asset's cost. These benefits must be calculated by a qualified accountant to ensure they apply to the specific business situation.

Fees, Insurance and Administrative Costs

Administrative and protective costs are unavoidable parts of the finance equation. Lenders charge establishment fees to process the application and secure the funds. These fees vary but cover the administrative work involved in assessing the business and registering the security interest over the asset. In addition to lender fees, borrowers must factor in the cost of comprehensive insurance. Lenders require this to ensure the asset is protected against damage or loss for the duration of the loan. This insurance cost is an ongoing expense that must be budgeted for annually. Finally, while some lenders charge early exit fees if the loan is paid out before the term ends, many commercial products offer flexibility, allowing businesses to settle early without penalty if their cash flow improves.

Common questions

What is the typical deposit required for equipment finance? Deposits typically sit between 10 and 30 per cent of the asset value. However, established businesses with strong trading histories may be eligible for 100 per cent financing, requiring no deposit.

Can I claim the interest costs on my taxes? Yes, for structures like chattel mortgages and commercial hire purchases, the interest portion of the repayments is generally tax-deductible. You may also be able to claim depreciation and the GST paid on the purchase.

How does a balloon payment affect the total cost? A balloon payment reduces your monthly instalments by deferring a portion of the principal to the end of the term. While this helps with monthly cash flow, you will generally pay more in total interest over the life of the loan.
